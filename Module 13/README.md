# Module 13 - Ethical AI and Future Trends

**Course:** ITAI 4370 - AI in 5G and 6G Communications and ORAN Networks  
**Student:** Monica Joya  |  **Term:** Summer 2026

## Overview

Module 13 covered using AI responsibly rather than building it. It went over what ethical AI means, the principles behind it, the main frameworks and organizations shaping it, and how laws like the EU AI Act are starting to regulate it. This matters for telecom, where AI already steers networks that carry everyone's data.

## Artifacts in this folder

- [`Assignment 04/Joya_Assignment_ITAI4370.pdf`](Assignment%2004/Joya_Assignment_ITAI4370.pdf) - A two-part assignment: conceptual questions and applied scenarios, cited in APA.

## Problem statement

Explain the core ideas of ethical AI, then apply them to real scenarios such as a hiring tool that favors one group over another.

## Methods and tools

Research and written analysis grounded in the module material and standards sources (IEEE Ethically Aligned Design, OECD, UNESCO, the EU AI Act), cited in APA.

## Results and interpretation

The conceptual section covered fairness, transparency, accountability, and privacy, plus algorithmic bias with a real example. The scenario section applied these to cases like a recruitment tool that favored male candidates, which violates fairness and accountability.

## What I learned

I learned that transparency and explainability are not the same: transparency is being open about how a system works, while explainability is being able to say, in plain terms, why it made a specific decision. I also learned that the EU AI Act sets rules by risk level, from banned uses like social scoring, through tightly controlled high-risk uses like hiring and credit, down to low-risk tools with no obligations, so the more harm something could do, the stricter the rules.
