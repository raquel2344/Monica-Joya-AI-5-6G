# Reflection 3: Skills I Improved and Areas for Future Growth

Looking back over the whole term, the clearest way to measure what I gained is to compare where I started with what I can now do on my own. A few skills stand out, and a few clear directions for growth came out of the work.

## Skills I improved

**Practical machine learning.** I went from never having built a model to building and comparing several: Random Forests for prediction, Isolation Forest and DBSCAN for anomaly detection, K-means for segmentation, and ARIMA, Linear Regression, and an LSTM for forecasting. More important than the count is that I learned the parts around the model that actually decide whether it works, especially feature engineering and choosing the right metric, like using precision, recall, and F1 for rare events instead of plain accuracy.

**Honest evaluation.** This is the skill I am most glad I built. Catching the target leakage in Module 06 changed how I read every result after it. I now treat a result that looks too good as a question rather than an answer, and I document problems instead of hiding them. In a field where models increasingly make real decisions, being able to trust my own numbers is worth more than any single technique.

**Reading and fixing other people's code.** Several labs handed me starter code that was broken or written for an older library version. Fixing the knowledge distillation in Module 07, updating the removed Mesa scheduler in the midterm, and correcting a demand loop in the Module 04 lab all taught me to work inside an existing codebase, find the real problem, make the smallest correct change, and mark what I changed and why.

**Explaining technical work in plain language.** Every module in this portfolio is written to be understood, not to sound impressive. Turning a lab result into a clear, honest paragraph is a skill I practiced deliberately all term, and it is one I expect to use in every technical role.

**Working across the telecom and AI stack.** I can now move from the physical layer, tracing a signal and modeling path loss, up through the network layer, reading captures and understanding the 5G core, and into the application of AI on top of all of it. Seeing the whole stack, rather than one slice of it, is what lets me reason about where AI actually helps.

## Areas for future growth

**Edge deployment.** The edge computing lab in Module 07 was the part I most want to take further. Compressing a model so it runs on a small device with little memory and power is a real, practical problem, and I only scratched the surface of quantization, pruning, and distillation. I want to go from simulating this to actually deploying a compressed model on real hardware.

**Working with real network and sensor data.** Every dataset in the course was synthetic, which was the right choice for learning, but the next step is messy real data. I want to build the skills to clean, join, and model live network and sensor streams, where the patterns are less tidy and the failures are more interesting.

**Toward digital energy.** The threads I care most about, edge AI, sensor data, and the honest evaluation of models, all point toward the kind of digital and data work happening in the energy sector. My goal is to keep building in that direction, using what this course taught me about modeling network and sensor data as a bridge into digital energy roles, where good logging and good models both matter.

## Closing

The competency this course built in me is not a list of libraries. It is the ability to take a real problem, pick the right method, be honest about the result, and explain it clearly, and to do that across a stack I understood nothing about three months ago. That is the foundation I plan to keep building on.
